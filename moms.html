COVID-19 and Women

The Value of Invisible Work in the Age of the Coronavirus

As work from home policies become widespread, household's divisions of labor are getting recallibrated. This time around, they are even more unfair to women.

By Amanda Glodowski and MinJi Koo

May 12, 2020

Claire Song, a web designer in Hoboken, New Jersey, spent a lot of time at home doing her work last week as usual. Since the coronavirus changed the landscape of working places – the increase of home working, she was not the only one who spent the whole week at home. Her husband, who works for Logistics, also stayed at home. While both of her and her husband worked at home, she cooked three times a day, did laundry on Wednesday and Friday, and vacuumed the house every other day.

“Definitely I have more daily chores these days,” said Song. “One thing that has never changed. I’m the one who’s taking more time for domestic work.” Song is not the only woman who spends more time for household chores under the stay-at-home orders."

Lockdowns amid the coronavirus left many people to work from home, be furloughed or be unemployed. Parents need to be teachers and playmates for their children because schools close. The burdens of cooking, cleaning, childcare and every necessary works for keeping a household running are largely falling on women in heterosexual couples.

“This is a total disaster to moms like me,” said Stella Wayne, a mother of two kids who works as an office worker at a tax account office in Orlando, Florida. She said that she is not the only mom who’s juggling childcare and work at home after children stopped going to school due to the pandemic. Because she is the most skilled at cooking and cleaning, she usually manages more household affairs than any others at home. “My husband helps me to manage all the things, but literally, he helps. Not sharing.”

OECD data has already showed that women in the United States spend 1.6 times more than men for unpaid work; men spend about 17.5 hours a week, and women spend about 28.4 hours a week doing unpaid labor. Also, the United Nations also reported that women do 2.6 times much unpaid domestic works and caregiving than men in heterosexual partners.

The Bureau of Statistics’ 2019 data gives a hunch about how much women can be compensated for their “invisible works” at home by suggesting the average payment of maids and housekeeping cleaners. For instance, national average payment for housekeeping professionals is $11.84 an hour, and for childcare workers is $10.18 an hour. This means that if Song spent 3 hours for cooking, 2 hours for doing laundry, and an hour for cleaning her house on last Wednesday, her time and effort on that day was worth the housekeepers’ pay of $71.04. Because Song said that she spends at least 3 more hours a day keeping her households running, the compensation for her unpaid labor amid the pandemic should increase as much as the worth of $35.52.
